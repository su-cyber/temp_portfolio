Introducing ROSE: Transforming Elderly Care

About ROSE

ROSE is an innovative care robot developed by Heemskerk Innovative Technology to support elderly care facilities. Designed to serve as both a nursing assistant and nutritionist, ROSE provides comprehensive support through:

Health Monitoring

Tracking vital signs and detecting changes in residents' health conditions

Nutrition Management

Monitoring meals and dietary requirements to ensure proper nutrition

Medication Adherence

Reminding residents to take medications at scheduled times

Social Interaction

Providing companionship and emotional support through conversation

By automating these essential daily tasks, ROSE significantly reduces staff workload while ensuring residents receive consistent, personalized care tailored to their individual needs.

Essential Building Blocks

From our mindmap, we identified several core components essential for ROSE's functionality:

  • Mood Detection: Sensors that analyze facial expressions, speech patterns, and vital signs
  • Emotional Expression: Simple display mechanisms to convey ROSE's emotional state
  • Comfort Systems: Calming sounds, ambient lighting, and voice interactions
  • Decision Logic: Systems to determine appropriate responses to various situations
  • Memory Functions: Tracking changes in resident conditions over time
  • Communication Tools: Video call interfaces to connect residents with families

These elements transform ROSE from a simple machine into a compassionate companion that actively listens and responds to residents' needs.

Functional Breakdown

We deconstructed ROSE's capabilities into manageable components to explore early prototyping possibilities:

Mood Tracking

Understanding patient emotional states through various indicators

Nutritional Guidance

Suggesting appropriate meals and beverages based on health needs

Alert Systems

Notifying staff when intervention is required

Comfort Features

Using lights and sounds to create calming environments

Many of these functions can be effectively prototyped using Wizard-of-Oz techniques, where human operators control aspects of ROSE's behavior behind the scenes during testing phases.

Experimental Approaches

We explored several innovative methods to enhance ROSE's capabilities:

  • Mood Detection Tests: ROSE asks residents about their emotional state and adapts behavior accordingly
  • Empathy Comparisons: Contrasting user reactions to task-focused versus emotionally-responsive versions
  • Prototyping Tools: Using Scratch programming and puppeteering to simulate comforting features
  • Feedback Systems: Simulating multi-day mood tracking to enable earlier interventions

These experiments provide valuable insights into which emotional and social features truly matter in care environments.

Design Reflections

Reflection #1: The Heart of HRI

Design research feels like the core of Human-Robot Interaction. It's not just about creating functional robots, but understanding how people experience and relate to them. It's easy to become absorbed in technology and overlook the human element. Design research compels us to pause, observe, and listen: How do people genuinely feel around robots? What do they expect? It's about balancing technical capabilities with human needs to foster comfort, respect, and emotional connection.

How design research connects to HRI: We often assume robots require physical forms to feel "real," but digital assistants challenge this notion. Personality and interaction style sometimes matter more than physical shape. Yet, interacting with an embodied robot creates a distinct sense of presence. Embodiment adds a connection layer difficult to replicate with voice or text alone, even with identical AI. Both approaches have value, with effectiveness measured by how well they meet human social needs.

Digital vs Embodiment: The film "Her" profoundly redefines what "being with" someone means. The concept of a disembodied AI as a true companion is powerful and beautiful, showing connection transcends physical presence. However, it also raises skepticism - would this work beyond cinema? Humans rely on subtle nonverbal cues to build trust and bonds. Without a body, can AI truly bridge these gaps, or is it merely an appealing illusion?

Why "Her" presents an incomplete argument: I once believed the robot revolution was imminent, but reality is more complex. While technology advances rapidly, human acceptance and trust develop slowly. Robots aren't mere gadgets; they're social entities needing to integrate into our messy, emotional human world. The transformation occurs gradually, almost quietly, in healthcare, factories, and homes. It's less a sudden leap and more a careful interplay between technological progress and social adaptation.

The Henn na Hotel Experiment

The Henn na Hotel's robot staff experiment offered fascinating insights into automation limits. While a fully robotic hotel seemed futuristic and exciting, many robots struggled with basic tasks and disappointed guests. This demonstrated that technology alone is insufficient - human empathy and flexibility remain crucial, especially in hospitality. The hotel's decision to reintroduce human staff alongside robots underscores the importance of balancing automation with human touch for meaningful service experiences.

Nabaztag: An Early Vision

Nabaztag represented an early dream of the social internet, giving digital life a physical presence. Ahead of its time, it aspired to be a social companion but faced technological limitations and user uncertainty. Its novelty faded quickly due to lacking deeper meaning and adaptability. Nabaztag reminds us that social robots need ongoing relevance and authentic interaction beyond their form.

Cuddlebits: The Power of Touch

Cuddlebits highlighted the significance of touch and play, often overlooked in robotics. It revealed how physical interaction creates emotional bonds impossible through screens or voice alone. Research showed that robots integrated into our tactile daily lives can offer genuine, lasting comfort and connection - a powerful insight for social technology design.

Be Right Back: Emotional Boundaries

"Be Right Back" resonated personally as a poignant exploration of technology attempting and failing to replace human loss. It revealed the dangers of expecting too much from AI or robots to fill emotional voids. The episode challenges us to distinguish between authentic and simulated experiences and consider their impact on wellbeing. It's a crucial reminder to design with care, respect, and honesty, recognizing that robots should support human life, not imitate it.
