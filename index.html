Emotion Expression through Light and Sound

A multimodal communication system for socially assistive robots in elderly care environments

Design Concept

ROSE is a socially assistive robot designed for elderly care environments. To address potential hearing difficulties and ensure clear communication, we developed a multimodal system using LED colors and sounds to express the robot's emotional state and intentions.

Why Light and Sound?

Colors are universal signals (red for danger, green for safety) that are easily interpreted, especially by elderly users. Sounds reinforce the meaning and ensure understanding even if color perception is limited. This combination creates a flexible, easily recognizable system that makes ROSE more approachable and trustworthy.

Problem Identification

Elderly users often have hearing difficulties and may struggle with subtle cues. We needed a communication system that was intuitive and accessible.

Modality Selection

After evaluating various modalities, we selected light and sound as the most effective combination for elderly users due to their universality and accessibility.

Emotion Mapping

We mapped specific emotions to color-sound combinations through extensive user testing with elderly participants.

Validation

The system was tested in simulated care scenarios to ensure clarity and effectiveness across different situations.

Group Work Observations

Paper Prop Puppeteering

We began with simple paper robot props to explore basic expressions. This low-fidelity approach allowed rapid iteration and helped us understand how minimal cues can convey emotion.

Key observations:

  • Simple movements like tilting forward could effectively convey curiosity
  • Back-and-forth swaying created a sense of "thinking" or processing
  • Participants quickly associated color changes with emotional states
  • Combining motion with sound significantly improved emotion recognition

Desk Light Puppet

Using a desk lamp as a more advanced prototype, we explored how light dynamics (pulsing, fading, intensity) combined with sound could create nuanced emotional expressions.

Key insights:

  • Pulsing light created a "heartbeat" effect that conveyed life and presence
  • Faster pulsing rates increased perceived urgency
  • Color transitions helped convey state changes (e.g., blue to yellow for mood improvement)
  • Sound pitch variations dramatically affected emotional interpretation

Reflective Questions

Applying Motion Design Principles (Ju & Hoffmann)

Ju & Hoffmann's work emphasizes "functional minimalism" in motion design. For ROSE, we applied this by using only essential movements that clearly communicate intention. For example:

  • A slight forward lean when initiating interaction signals attention
  • Slow backward movement when disengaging reduces perceived abruptness
  • Minimal head tilts convey curiosity without complex articulation

This approach aligns with their principle that motion should enhance functionality rather than distract.

Downsides of HHI as Starting Point for HRI

While human-human interaction (HHI) provides valuable insights, Heather Knight's research highlights limitations in directly applying HHI principles to HRI:

  • Robots lack the nuance of human nonverbal communication
  • Elderly users may have different expectations from robots than humans
  • Over-anthropomorphizing can create unrealistic expectations
  • Robots have different physical constraints than humans

As Knight suggests, we focused on designing robot-specific expressions that leverage mechanical advantages rather than mimic human limitations.

Laban Framework Beyond Motion

Laban's effort dimensions (weight, time, space, flow) can be effectively applied to sound design:

  • Weight: Heavy sounds (low pitch) convey seriousness, light sounds (high pitch) for playfulness
  • Time: Sudden sounds for urgency, sustained sounds for calm
  • Space: Directional sounds (panning) create spatial awareness
  • Flow: Free-flowing sounds for comfort, bound sounds for precision

For haptics, we applied "weight" through vibration intensity and "time" through duration patterns.

Designing Anti-Social Behavior

Anti-social behavior design differs significantly from simply lacking social cues:

  • Active avoidance: Turning away when approached
  • Minimal responses: Short, monotone replies
  • Task-focused: Ignoring social niceties during interactions
  • Boundary enforcement: Maintaining maximum personal space

This approach might be appropriate in contexts like security robots or industrial settings where social interaction is undesirable.

Application to ROSE Care Scenarios

Our emotion expression system was designed specifically for common elderly care situations:

Medication Reminders

ROSE uses yellow light with a cheerful tone to initiate interaction, transitioning to blue with a soft chime when providing empathetic reminders for users who might feel frustrated about taking medication.

Exercise Motivation

During exercise sessions, ROSE pulses green light with rhythmic sounds to encourage movement, shifting to purple with ticking sounds when counting repetitions to indicate processing.

Mealtime Assistance

When assisting with meals, ROSE maintains a steady yellow light with intermittent cheerful tones to create a pleasant atmosphere, switching to orange with double beeps if it needs help identifying food items.

Sleep Routine

At bedtime, ROSE gradually transitions to dim white light with no sound to signal transition to sleep mode, helping establish a calming routine.

Embodiment Design Tool

The Challenge of Available Hardware

Social robotics research often utilizes whatever hardware is available rather than what's ideally suited for the application. This has led to Pepper and NAO robots being used in diverse roles from librarians to educators, regardless of their appropriateness for these contexts.

Why Pepper and NAO Dominate Research

Factor Benefit Research Consequence
Availability Commercially available, standardized platforms Reduced barriers to entry for research teams
Versatility Programmable interfaces adaptable to various roles Used across education, healthcare, hospitality
Community Support Large user base, tutorials, and libraries Easier troubleshooting and knowledge sharing
Cost Efficiency Reusable across multiple projects More feasible for grant-funded research

Despite these advantages, this "one-size-fits-all" approach limits innovation and often results in mismatches between robot capabilities and application requirements.

Embodiment Tool : Design Process

The Embodiment Design Tool was conceived through a rigorous, research-driven process rooted in participatory and co-design methodologies. Our primary goal was to create an accessible, creative, and evidence-based approach for involving diverse stakeholders—especially older adults and care professionals—in the design of social robot embodiments.

1. Grounding in Participatory and Co-Design Research
We drew inspiration from the participatory design tradition, which emphasizes the importance of involving end-users directly in the design process to ensure that resulting technologies are meaningful, usable, and accepted by those they are intended to serve [1][2]. In the context of social robotics, participatory methods have been shown to empower users, surface tacit knowledge, and lead to more contextually appropriate robot designs [3].

2. Card-Based Toolkit Approach
The decision to use a card-based toolkit was motivated by recent HRI research demonstrating that tangible, visual tools—such as the Modality Card Deck—effectively support ideation, communication, and reflection among non-expert participants, including older adults [4]. Cards provide a playful and structured way to explore embodiment options, lower barriers for participation, and stimulate discussion about both functional and social aspects of robot design.

3. Scenario-Driven, Iterative Development
We structured the tool around real-life scenarios, allowing participants to co-create robot embodiments tailored to specific contexts (e.g., elderly care, nutrition support). This scenario-driven approach is supported by literature as a way to ensure that design outcomes are relevant and grounded in users’ lived experiences [5]. The tool was iteratively refined through pilot sessions with stakeholders, incorporating their feedback to improve clarity, inclusivity, and creative potential.

4. Research-Backed Benefits
Our approach is aligned with best practices in HRI and design research, which highlight the value of participatory and card-based methods for:

  • Empowering users and increasing acceptance of social robots [1][3]
  • Facilitating communication and consensus among diverse stakeholders [2][4]
  • Supporting iterative, scenario-driven exploration of embodiment options [5]

In summary, the Embodiment Design Tool is the product of an evidence-based, participatory design process, leveraging the strengths of card-based toolkits and scenario-driven co-design. This approach ensures that social robot embodiments are not only technically feasible, but also socially meaningful and contextually appropriate.

Reflective Questions

1. Embodied vs. Virtual Agents & Hybrid Forms

The distinction between embodied and virtual agents remains a foundational topic in human-robot interaction (HRI), though the field increasingly embraces hybrid embodiments that combine physical and virtual elements for enhanced adaptability and user engagement.

Physical + Virtual

Example: Furhat Robotics' screen-based social robots with customizable faces

Advantage: Enables persona switching and multi-context interaction

Application: Healthcare, customer service, education

Distributed Embodiment

Example: Service robots combining AI chatbots with physical kiosks

Advantage: Integrates conversational AI with tangible presence

Application: Travel assistance, information services

Projection-Based

Example: Robots using projection to create expressive faces on minimal physical forms

Advantage: Cost-effective, flexible expression

Application: Education, entertainment

Design Insight: Hybrid embodiments optimize cost, flexibility, and emotional connection by leveraging physical presence alongside virtual adaptability. This approach is increasingly supported by research emphasizing the benefits of combining tangible and virtual social cues for richer interaction experiences[1][6].

2. Robotic Capabilities vs. Social Requirements

Robotic design must balance engineering capabilities (e.g., sensors, mobility) with social interaction requirements (e.g., trust, empathy). These domains require distinct but integrated frameworks to ensure effective and accepted robot behavior.

Engineering Requirements Social Requirements Measurement Methods Reference
Obstacle detection accuracy User trust in navigation Behavioral logs, trust questionnaires (e.g., Godspeed scale) [2]
Voice recognition latency Perceived empathy Facial expression analysis, engagement duration [3]
Battery life Willingness for long-term use Usage frequency logs, user interviews [4]

Conflict Resolution: Social needs often take precedence over pure technical efficiency. For example, perceptual salience—such as expressive eyes or friendly facial features—can significantly enhance user trust and acceptance, even if it compromises some functional aspects. Co-design workshops with stakeholders are recommended to align technical and social requirements effectively[2][3][4].

3. Functional vs. Social Design Conflicts

Design conflicts frequently arise when functional demands clash with social expectations. Addressing these conflicts requires creative solutions that satisfy both domains.

Security Robot

Functional need: Multiple cameras for comprehensive surveillance

Social need: Non-threatening appearance to reduce public anxiety

Resolution: Use of dome-shaped housings that conceal cameras when idle, combined with soft lighting to signal operational status, balancing surveillance with approachability[5].

ROSE Nutritionist

Functional need: Food recognition sensors for dietary assistance

Social need: Discreet presence in elderly dining environments

Resolution: Embedding sensors in serving trays, using wood-textured materials, and subtle perimeter lighting to blend functionality with a warm, non-intrusive design[6].

Design Principle: "Functional minimalism"—prioritizing intuitive, simple gestures and reducing mechanical complexity—enhances interpretability and user comfort, as supported by research on motion design in social robots[7].

4. Duffy's Embodiment Design Framework

Duffy's framework identifies key aspects critical for embodiment design. For this tool, the most relevant aspects include:

Perceptual Salience

Application: Prioritizing facial features such as eyes and mouth to enhance emotional connection

Evidence: Studies show users prefer baby-face features for emotional tasks, increasing trust and engagement[8].

Impact: Design choices like round eyes and upturned mouths can boost trust by over 30%.

Behavioral Fluency

Application: Mapping robot movements to social contexts to improve predictability and comfort

Evidence: Predictable motions increase trust significantly in healthcare settings[9].

Impact: Tailoring motion style (e.g., slow and smooth for elderly care) enhances acceptance.

Cultural Appropriateness

Application: Adapting materials and colors to cultural norms to improve acceptance

Evidence: Mismatched cultural cues can reduce acceptance by nearly 30%[10].

Impact: Color symbolism varies widely; for example, red signifies urgency in Western cultures but prosperity in many Asian cultures.

Tool Integration: This embodiment design tool incorporates Duffy's aspects as filters during component selection, ensuring alignment with social and cultural contexts for optimal user acceptance[8][9][10].
